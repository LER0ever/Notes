    Math240-02 Propositional Logic and Equivalent

    Propositions

    A proposition is a declarative sentence (that is, a sentence that declares a fact) that is either true or false, but not both. (T || F)

    Ex:
    1. Madison is the capital of Wisconsin --> T
    2. 1 + 2 = 3 --> T
    3. 1 + 4 = 9 --> F
    Non Ex:
    1. What time is it?
    2. Read a book
    3. X + 1 = 5
    4. X + Y = Z

    Let p, q, r … be propositional varaible

    Ex. Let p be the prop. “1 + 2 = 3”

    Ex. Let q be the prop. “1 + 7 = 11”

    Negation

    Negation of a proposition p, denoted by ¬p, is the proposition “It is not the case that P” or “Not P”

    Ex. P be I have a phone --> It is not the case that I have a phone --> I don’t have a phone

    True value to ¬p is opposite of that of p

    p¬p
    TF
    FT

    Ex. p: My phone has at least 36 GB of storage. --> ¬p: It is not the case that my phone has at least 36 GB of storage --> ¬p: My phone has less than 36 GB of storage

    Ex. p: For all p > 0, there is a delta > 0 S.T. for all x and y with |x-y| < delta, a particular function f satisfies |f(x) - f(y)| < p, then f is differentiable.

    ¬p is hard

    Conjunction

    Let p and q be propositions. The conjunction of p and q , denoted by p ∧ q , is the proposition “p and q .” The conjunction p ∧ q is true when both p and q are true and is false otherwise.

    Ex. 7+2=9 ∧ 2=3

    p qp ∧ q
    T TT
    T FF
    F TF
    F FF

    Disjuncion

    Let p and q be propositions. The disjunction of p and q , denoted by p ∨ q , is the proposition “p or q .” The disjunction p ∨ q is false when both p and q are false and is true otherwise.

    Exclusive or:

    Let p and q be propositions. The exclusive or of p and q , denoted by p ⊕ q , is the proposition that is true when exactly one of p and q is true and is false otherwise.

    Ex. Soup or salad comes with the sandwiches

    Conditional Statement

    Let p and q be propositions. The conditional statement p → q is the proposition “if p, then q .” The conditional statement p → q is false when p is true and q is false, and true otherwise. In the conditional statement p → q , p is called the hypothesis (or antecedent or premise) and q is called the conclusion (or consequence).

    Alternative statement @Page 6@

    Ex. If 7 = 2, then I am a bird --> T

    Ex. If 7 != 2, then I am not a bird --> T

    Ex. If Mary has a smart phone, then 2 + 3 = 5 --> T

    Ex. p: If Mary has a smart phone, then 2 + 3 = 7. p is false if Mary has a smart phone. But if Mary doesn’t have a smart phone, p is true.

    Ex, If you give me $100, I’ll smile. --> T

    p → q --> q → p, converse

    Ex. If I smile, then you give me $100

    The proposition q → p is called the converse of p → q . The contrapositive of p → q is the proposition ¬q →¬p . The proposition ¬p →¬q is called the inverse of p → q .
